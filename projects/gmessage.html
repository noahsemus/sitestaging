A slight preamble to the project: GMessage is project borne out of pure passion and utter frustration. I am not shy about my love for Google, and I have always had a passion about making ubiquitous experiences (like messaging) easier and more fun to use. Google's troubles in this space opened up a perfect opportunity for me. There's the passion. The frustration has come from my junior year of education at Pratt Institute. Wanting to be an interactive designer, I've found Pratt almost refuses to teach me and give me projects that I think are important to becoming well versed in the digital space. Therefore, I created my own project -- not only to teach myself, but to show I know what I can do. Along with the screen exports here is the annotated deck of designs if you really want to pick my brain.

Anyways, here's wonderwall.

Stage 1:

We have to identify the main problem. Why haven't Google's past messaging services worked?

Problem:

Most users are hard set on their current messaging apps. Getting them to switch can be a pain.

Solution:

Integrate the service into something everybody uses -- Google Chrome & the standard Android messenger.

Thought Process:

Via user research, many people stated they used Facebook's system simply because people are on Facebook. By utilizing Chrome, the web's most popular browser by a wide margin, we are beginning to integrate this messenger into a product people use on the daily.

Stage 2:

Wireframing the app. It is designed on an 8 dp grid with 16 dp margins.

After the initial ideation and brief user testing, I soon realized that while I was showcasing the most recent/important content up top, I was then making it the least reachable content on the screen. I then created heat maps of areas that are hard to reach based off of two different hand positions, one where I found users held the phone in the middle, and one where they held from the bottom and propped it up with their pinky.

From this, I added the google assistant to give users relevant info while keeping content in easy to reach areas.

For the messaging interface I focused on the send bar. Should the send button be inside or out? After a focus group, most people had identified the send button making more sense outside of the send bar, as they prefer to keep attachments within the message; they see them as part of the send message form itself rather than a separate entity. This also works in highlighting the send action as an important CTA when activated.

As stated before the desktop integrates directly into chrome, and acts as a slide out panel. As such, the design translates fairly straightforwardly due to the similar aspect ratios.

Stage 3:

Visual design. Here we are able to add colour and branding to make the app feel fun as well as add user freedom with customizable colours.

Within the visual design phase, the messaging screens were built out even more to include group chats and sms versus standard cloud states.

I discovered some features might be slighter harder for a user to understand or naturally discover -- these will be explained quickly and concisely within the onboarding process. Pinning will be explained fluidly after a small period of use. (Onboarding illustrations done by the talented Jooyoung Park)

The desktop translation is once again fairly straightforward, and it ultimately holds closely to the original wireframes. Again it is easily integrated with chrome.

The entire app was also built within a modular system, so colours and layouts can be changed easily at will. This of course allows for easy colour treatment and dark mode integration.

Stage 4:

The final stage (I say final, but of course this was a circular design process) is prototyping. Animations are incredibly important to the feel and experience of the app. The standard messaging app uses system animations to go from state to state, and this can truly break the experience.
